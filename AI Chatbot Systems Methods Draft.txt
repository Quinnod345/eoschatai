SYSTEMS AND METHODS FOR ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE CHATBOT IN ENTREPRENEURIAL MANAGEMENT

FIELD
[0001] Embodiments of the subject matter disclosed herein relate to chatbots, and more specifically to generating company-specific responses with an artificial intelligence chatbot for entrepreneurial management. 

BACKGROUND
[0002] Business and entrepreneurial management is a framework that aims to help entrepreneurs, business owners, and leadership teams clarify, simplify, and achieve their vision. For example, such a business management system may provide practical tools and a structured approach for a business that focuses on strengthening various aspects of the business, such as the business's vision, the business's people (e.g., employees, management team staff, etc.), and more. Through structured implementation of tools by the business management system, such as a vision/traction organizer (V/TO), meetings, accountability charts, and the like, businesses can be guided to a clear set of goals, track performance, and build a cohesive leadership team aligned around a shared vision. 
[0003] While such business management frameworks address common issues that plague growing business, such as a lack of direction, poor communication, performance dips, and leadership misalignment, formulating business-specific implementation plans and providing business-specific guidance for various types of clients is difficult due to the varying industry standards, business goals, management structures, and the like. Additionally, the stage of a particular business may affect how the business/entrepreneurial management framework is applied and implemented. For example, implementations of tools and methodologies by the business management system may be quite different for a startup business compared to a well-established franchise. Thus, providing a consistent process across clientele becomes challenging. 
[0004] The use of artificial intelligence (AI) chatbots in place of human correspondence or to supplement human interaction is an increasingly popular option to reduce costs and improve efficiency in addressing customer or client queries and interactions. For customer or client-focused companies, users may choose to interact with AI-based chatbots for answering common questions or obtaining general information rather than spending the time to schedule an appointment and meeting with a company employee. Thus, the chatbot is a user-friendly contact option for clients, providing clients with answers to general queries in a timely and efficient manner. In recent years, the chatbot has become an efficient interactive tool and method to provide services to clients. However, such generic chatbots are unable to provide user and company specific questions for clients. 
[0005] A company-specific chatbot addresses at least some of these issues by providing a background framework that is aligned with the specific intentions of the company. Thus, systems and methods for a company-specific AI chatbot for entrepreneurial management are herein provided. The chatbot herein described is trained on not only the business/entrepreneurial management system's internal data (e.g., the implementation tools, methodologies, and the like) that make up the knowledge base for helping clients (e.g., businesses, start-ups, management teams, etc.), but also the client's specific data and persona data of individual users, which includes both client users and system users (e.g., implementers of the business management system). In this way, the chatbot may be configured to generate responses to user inputted queries that are rooted in the business management system's internal knowledge base of methodologies and tools as well as being specific to the relevant client and the user who is inputting the query. 

BRIEF DESCRIPTION
[0006] Methods and systems are herein provided for a company-specific chatbot system. In one example, a computing device comprises a chatbot system comprising a plurality of retrieval-augmented generators (RAGs); and one or more processors configured to execute instructions stored in memory that when executed cause the one or more processors to: receive a user query input to the chatbot system; retrieve relevant client-specific data, relevant system specific data, and relevant persona data with the plurality of RAGs in a parallel processing manner; and generate a response to the user query input based on the retrieved relevant client-specific data, retrieved relevant system specific data, and retrieved relevant persona data.

DETAILED DESCRIPTION
[0017] The following description relates to systems and methods for a chatbot system. In particular, systems and methods are provided for automatically generating a response to a user inputted query using an AI-based chatbot. The systems and methods herein facilitate AI-based responses to user queries that are specific both to the user and to an entity with which the user is utilizing or otherwise communicating with. 
[0018] Business management and operational frameworks have become essential tools for helping businesses systematically improve their performance and achieve strategic objectives. However, implementing such frameworks effectively presents certain challenges. Traditional implementation relies heavily on human consultants and coaches (herein referred to as implementers) who can work with only a limited number of clients, leading to scheduling delays, inconsistent guidance, and high costs. Additionally, business clients often struggle to receive timely answers to implementation questions between implementer sessions, potentially stalling their progress or leading to improper application of the framework principles. 
[0019] The disclosed chatbot system is herein described with respect to such a business management system that includes a plurality of human implementers for working with clients. As described, the clients, such as small business owners, startup companies, and the like, often have in-person or virtual sessions with an assigned implementer, whereby the implementer uses company/system methodologies and business management tools to help the client manage their business in a variety of ways. However, this implementer-client process can be time consuming to set up meetings and the implementer may have multiple clients at once, making sorting through client-specific documents, keeping track of where each client is in a given method, and the like difficult and prone to human error. In some examples, a client may have a quick question about a methodology or a next step that may not necessitate a scheduled meeting with an implementer. 
[0020] The disclosed chatbot system is designed to provide expert-level guidance for business management framework implementation. The system employs a sophisticated three-tier architecture combining retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) technology with specialized AI personas that can adapt responses based on a client's specific context, industry, size, and implementation stage. The system provides for methodology-compliant guidance while personalizing recommendations to each client's specific situation. This allows clients to receive immediate, consistent, and contextually-appropriate support without having to wait for implementer availability. For example, the chatbot system can quickly provide answers to user queries that are rooted both in the company's own methodologies as well as client-specific data. Thus, both client users and system users (e.g., implementers) can utilize the chatbot for answering user queries, planning next meeting sessions, and more. '
[0021] By democratizing access to expert-level implementation guidance, the disclosed systems and methods transforms how a business management system can implement its frameworks and how clients of such a business management system can adopt and benefit from the business management frameworks.

[0025] The client-specific data 110 may comprise stored documents, metadata, and the like that are uploaded to the system 100 by clients. This may include business specific documents, employee schema, goals, plans, strategies, and more. The system specific data 112 may comprise methodologies and other business management tools that are utilized, typically by human implementers, in working with clients. 
[0026] System specific data 112, such as methodologies and business management tools, are organized into specialized namespaces. For example, methodologies are organized in order based on how a system implementer of the methodologies would present them to a client. As a non-limiting example, a first namespace may be a general methodology namespace, a second namespace may be a "day 1" namespace, a third namespace may be a "day 2" namespace, a fourth namespace may be a "quarterly planning" namespace, and so on. The system may be configured to pull from these various namespaces depending on the user and the user inputted query. For example, a client user may be associated with a particular step of a methodology or tool (e.g., the client may be at "day 2" in their business management implementation), and thus in response to a user inputted query from said client, the chatbot system 108 may pull from the corresponding namespace (e.g., the "day 2" namespace). Further, the chatbot system 108 may use vector embeddings to create semantic clusters of related concepts, allowing for intelligent cross-referencing of similar business challenges and solutions. 
[0027] The client-specific data 110, such as client documents, may be categorized by file type, business area, and metadata tags during upload to the memory 106. The client-specific data 110 may be filtered or otherwise managed such that only data relevant to the current user and/or user inputted query are referenced when generating a response by the chatbot system 108. For example, when a client user inputs a query, only data (e.g., documents) relating to that client user may be pulled from. As another example, when a system user (e.g., an implementer), inputs a query, client-specifics may be determined from the query (e.g., via natural language processing) and thus client data related to that client may be pulled from. 
[0028] In search the client-specific data 110 and the system specific data 112 when generating a response to a user inputted query, the chatbot system 108 may use hierarchical navigable small world (HNSW) indexing for fast similarity searching operations. For example, when a profile is selected, a profile namespace may be searched first, and then if less than a threshold number of results the system then searches in a base namespace. The chatbot system 108 further utilizes chunking algorithms to split data documents for optimizing retrieval of information from documents for reduced processing demands. In addition, the chatbot system 108 may implement parallel processing, whereby multiple RAGs run in parallel, as will be described further below, and relevance filtering, wherein multiple threshold levels filter results by relevance score to ensure high-quality matches. 
[0029] In order to link the client-specific data and the system specific data when generating responses to user inputted queries, such that data that is relevant to a client is associated with relevant system tools and methodologies, the chatbot system 108 uses a three-tier RAG architecture, as will be further described below. Further, each client user's documents may be stored in an individual namespace (e.g., an upstash vector namespace) using a user identification code (e.g., a user ID) as the namespace identifier. Data may additionally be associated with users through metadata that includes the user ID, document IDs, file names, categories, file types, and the like. 
[0030] The system specific data 112 may define specific methodologies and tools, including the orders in which they are to take place. For example, each step of a given methodology may be tagged with a given order position, which then may be used by the chatbot system 108 when the system is generating a response to a user query. in this way, information about a methodology is presented to the user in the designated order and for the steps relevant to the user. 
[0031] The persona data 114 may store data relevant to individual users, including client users and system users (e.g., implementers). For example, data of how a user interacts with the chatbot (e.g., from feedback monitored by the chatbot system). Each user may have an associated persona namespace where data specific to that user is stored. For client users, this may differ from the client-specific data, which may be related to the overall client's business data (including current stage in a methodology, revenue, organization, etc.).

In some examples, the chatbot system 108 may search through client and system specific data in their existing forms and then translate the data formats into a format usable by the chatbot system 108 when pulling and processing the data for response generation.

In some examples, the generated output from the individual RAGs of the chatbot system 108 may be combined and processed into a final response output that is presented to the user (e.g., a client user or an implementer user).

[0043] The chatbot system herein described thus provides several advantages when implemented within a business management framework. First, by utilizing multiple RAGs in a parallel processing manner, the system can efficiently process and analyze large volumes of both client-specific business data and system base knowledge simultaneously. This parallel architecture allows the chatbot to generate responses that are tailored to each client's specific business context while ensuring adherence to the business management system's established methodologies and practices. 
[0044] Additionally, the system's three-tier RAG architecture enables dynamic knowledge integration between client-specific information (such as business goals, organizational structure, and performance metrics) and the business management system's tools and frameworks. For example, when a client user queries about implementing a particular business management tool, the system can automatically contextualize its guidance based on the client's current implementation stage, business size, industry, and past interactions with the methodologies. This ensures that recommendations are not only methodologically sound but also practically feasible given the client's specific circumstances. 
[0045] In a diagram depicted in FIG. 3, a neural network 300 for response generation is shown. In one example, the neural network 300 is included in the computing system 100 of FIGS. 1 and 2, more specifically as the language model 200 of the chatbot system 108 of computing system 100, though it should be appreciated that the neural network 300 may be implemented with other systems and components without departing form the scope of the present disclosure. 
[0046] The neural network 300 may comprise a plurality of interconnected neurons 301 (or nodes) arranged in layers. As used herein with respect to neurons 301, the term "layer" refers to a collection of simulated neurons 301. For example, an input layer 302 may comprise multiple neurons 301, and similarly, an output layer 306 and a hidden layer 304 may each comprise multiple neurons 301. The input layer 302 may be connected to the hidden layer 304 by a first set of connectors 303 and the hidden layer 304 may be connected to the output layer 306 by a second set of connectors 305. As such, data may propagate from the input layer 302, through the hidden layer 304, to the output layer 306. In one example, the neurons 301 in the hidden layer 304 receive inputs and/or activation functions from the input layer 302. These activation functions may alter an output of the neurons 301 in the hidden layer 304 to the neurons 301 in the output layer 306. 
[0047] Each of the neurons 301 in the input layer 302 may be connected to more than one neuron in the hidden layer 304 and each neuron in the hidden layer 304 may be connected to more than one neuron in the output layer 306. It will be appreciated that a number of neurons 301 in an adjacent layer which one neuron is connected to may vary from the number of connections shown in FIG. 3. Furthermore, the number of neurons 301 in each layer and a number of layers in each type of layer may vary from that shown in the non-limiting example of FIG. 3. 
[0048] In one example, the neural network 300 may be a feedforward neural network. In some examples, the neural network 300 may learn through back-propagation. To minimize total error, gradient descent may be used to adjust each weight in proportion to the derivative of the error with respect to that weight, provided the non-linear activation functions are differentiable. Also, global optimization methods may be used to train the weights of the neural network 300. 
[0049] To generate responses to input queries, queries may be input to the neural network 300 which results in a yield of a plurality of outputs (e.g., a response). More specifically, input layer 302 receives input which is relayed to the hidden layer 304. Each neuron of the input layer 302 is connected to each neuron of the hidden layer 304. The neurons of the hidden layer 304 receive one or more inputs and sums them to produce an output. The sums of each neuron are weighted and the sums are passed through a non-linear activation function. The output is passed on to each neuron of the output layer 306. 
[0050] It should be understood that each neuron of the neural network 300 may transmit a same input to multiple output connections. It should also be appreciated that, for simplicity, FIG. 3 depicts a small number of neurons in each layer and that in practice, the neural network 300 may include many thousands of neurons. Moreover, the neural network 300 is shown with one hidden layer but may include dozens or more hidden layers and may be adjusted, for example, to an optimal number based on the number of neurons in the output layer 306. As an example, the output generated at the output layer 306 may be a word adapted to be concatenate to input queries, delivered to the input layer 302 as paraphrases. A complete sentence may be generated as an output by iteratively consuming the neural network 300 for next-word suggestions. The initial input to the input layer 302 may also be a specific state or word that is predefined. 
[0051] As another example, the output may be a series of sentences rather than a single sentence, the series of sentences linked to one another by a common word or phrase as an input. Each associated sentence may be iteratively used as subsequent inputs, with the outputs linking the sentences to one another. The series of sentences may be linked in a manner that simulates conversation via iterative experiences that result in associated generation of the individual sentences. Furthermore, the output may be generated as list of relevant actions to instruct suitable successive steps in response to the input.
